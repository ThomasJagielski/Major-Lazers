Electrical Subsystem

Laser Detection

In order to detect when the key is pressed, we use a photodiode to detect the exposed laser. The photodiodes are on the top of the pipes. The circuit we use is a voltage divider in which the photodiode is the first “resistor” in series. In doing this, we can detect the voltage change when the laser is shining on the diode.

What we adjusted through testing: In order to make the photodiode focus on the light from the laser, we needed to add a light filter over the detector. In practice, we colored over the photodiode with an expo marker.

Sound Creation

Initially we planned on making a voltage controlled oscillator. However, due to complicated circuitry and higher voltages (16 and -16 V), we ended up using 555 timers.

We use 555 timers to generate the signals used as various tones. By changing the resistors values in surrounding circuitry, we can generate tones of various frequencies. The output signal from the 555 timer is a discrete high/low; however, the threshold signal is more sinusoidal. Therefore, we pass the threshold signal through an op-amp to have the tones at various frequencies. We used the more analog signal as it sounds less computerized.

We also made an analog ADSR (Attack, Delay, Sustain, Release). This circuit effectively controls the charge of a capacitor in order to make a time variant signal. In combination with the oscillators, we generate a tone that is scaled by the ADSR signal.

We pass all of the oscillating signals, the ADSR signal, and the photodiode signals to the Teensy. Based on the photodiodes that are detected, we output an audio tone that is the sum of the oscillators and is scaled by the ADSR signal.
